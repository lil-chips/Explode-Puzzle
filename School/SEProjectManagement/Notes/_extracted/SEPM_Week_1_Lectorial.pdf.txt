===== Page 25 =====
Project
Methodologies
There are many Project Methodologies - variations of a them - common principles
differently packaged:
▪ Traditional – Waterfall(-based)
▪ RUP (Rational Unified Process) introduced by Rational, a division of IBM
▪ AUP (Agile Unified Process) - simplified version of RUP
▪ Agile
▪ Lean
▪ Prince2
▪ …
They describe how the Software Development Life Cycles ( SDLC) should work, i.e.,
how the phases of SDLC should be organized
Project Methodology:
• A process that documents a series of steps and
procedures to bring about the successful completion of a
project.
• A series of steps through which the project progresses
===== Page 26 =====
Processes in SDLC
1. Initiating Processes - performed to define new project or phase of
existing project by obtaining authorisation to start the project or
phase
2. Planning Processes – performed to establish the total scope of the
effort, define and refine objectives, and develop the course of action
required to attain those objectives
3. Executing Processes – performed to complete the work defined in
the project management plan to satisfy the project specifications
4. Monitoring & Controlling Processes – performed to track, review,
and regulate the progress and performance of the project
5. Closing Processes – performed to finalise all activities across all
Project Management Process Groups to formally close the project,
phases, or contractual obligations
===== Page 27 =====
Waterfall
 Waterfall = planning projects fully,
then executing through phases
1956: the first mentioning of the idea
by Herbert D. Benington at the
Symposium on Advanced Programming
Methods for Digital Computers
1970: the first formal description of the
waterfall model in article by Winston W.
Royce “Managing the Development of
Large Software Systems”
1976: The earliest use of the term
"waterfall" may have been by Bell and
Thayer in their paper “Software
requirements: Are they really a
problem?”
===== Page 28 =====
Traditional Methodologies
• Linear sequential model
• Requirements need to be locked
• Development only starts after planning
• Output of one step acts as input of next step
• Still widely used
• Separate and distinct phases of specification and development
• Separate and distinct use/application of resources
• Waterfall
• PMI’s PMBOK - applying universal standards to waterfall project management
• PRINCE2 - waterfall project management methodology that includes
principles, themes and processes (more strict)
Not flexible to readily
incorporate changes
Inappropriate for version-wise
release method
===== Page 29 =====
Error correction cost
The failure / success cant be decided till too late.
Errors found in later phases difficult to correct…
Cost of error correction grows rapidly along the
project life-cycle

Agile
It encompasses set of exciting new practices and techniques that
make product development more cyclical (or iterative).
It relies on lean governance (management) as opposed to more
traditional techniques that rely on heavyweight governance.
Agile is all about empowering the team and getting closer to what
the customer wants.
A framework for delivering products quickly and efficiently.
As opposed to the standard
requirements-gathering, plan-driven
project development, the customer is
continually involved in Agile.

===== Page 34 =====
Vision & Scope Document / Project Charter
Both documents serve the same purpose:
• Collect all business requirements into a single deliverable that sets the
stage for subsequent development work
• Define the business case for the project in nontechnical language
(overlaps with Business Case document!).
Goal/aim of these documents are the same, difference is in the format and
focus on particular topics
→ We never use both documents in the same project
→ We have to decide at the very beginning, what format we follow
• Owner of this document: project’s executive sponsor, funding authority, …
• Creator: Business Analyst, but might be also Project Manager or Product Owner
Goals: Specify and justify the project

WBS: Traditional vs. Agile
WBS in Traditional / Waterfall  projects
▪ Very detailed
▪ Who is doing what and when
Agile: You start with a high level plan and end up with a detailed plan…
So can we have WBS in Agile?
WBS in Agile:
▪ Agile WBS == your release plan has to be detailed
▪ Dependencies among features/ epics
▪ Traceability among features, user stories, and the sprint tasks
▪ (!) No allocation in advance to particular team members
Can we do the same in
Agile?

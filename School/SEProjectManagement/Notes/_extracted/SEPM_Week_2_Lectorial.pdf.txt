Agile
===== Page 6 =====
Agile
It encompasses set of exciting new practices and techniques that
make product development more cyclical (or iterative).
It relies on lean governance (management) as opposed to more
traditional techniques that rely on heavyweight governance.
Agile is all about empowering the team and getting closer to what
the customer wants.
A framework for delivering products quickly and efficiently.
As opposed to the standard
requirements-gathering, plan-driven
project development, the customer is
continually involved in Agile.
===== Page 7 =====
Why Agile
was created?
It was a reaction against the rigid and “big upfront planning”
approach that many software developers and business
analysts thought stifled the development process and contact
with the customer.
Agile wasn’t a simple
software developer’s revolt.
While Agile became popular
with software developers
because it freed time for
creativity and drove the
decision-making process
lower, the principles also
made sense from a
business perspective.

===== Page 10 =====
Agile  vs. Scrum
Scrum is a particular variant of Agile
and the most popular variant today.
While Agile is more a set of principles,
Scrum puts principles into effect with well -
designed practices and techniques:
Agile may give you the basic orientation,
but Scrum lets you bring Agile to your
organisation and make it work.
While Agile suggests a need for good communication,
Scrum sets up rules for stand-up meetings and how they
should be conducted.
Where Agile discusses the need for iterative and
incremental development, Scrum brings those terms into
focus with well-defined sprints that represent a specific
iteration.
Where Agile suggests you track tasks to be performed
with lists, Scrum has specific techniques in place for doing
exactly that.

Product owner
• Establishes and communicates the product vision
• Achieves initial and on-going funding for the project
• Creates initial release plans
• Makes decision about when to create an official release:
it may not be desirable to release at the conclusion of
every increment
• Develops and maintains/prioritizes the Product Backlog,
as well as presents and explains it to the team
• Empowered to make decisions for all customers and users
• May attend sprint planning meeting and sprint review
meeting
===== Page 14 =====
Scrum Master
• Responsible for the process
• Responsible for maximising team productivity and
removing obstacles to progress
• Sets up and conducts meetings:
▪ Sprint Planning Meetings, during which the team expands
Product Backlog into Sprint Backlog
▪ Sprint Review Meetings, during which the newly developed
functionality is demonstrated
▪ Short daily stand-ups
▪ …
• Representative to management
• Representative to team
===== Page 15 =====
Scrum Team
During an iteration, the team selects and develops the highest -priority
features on the Product Backlog
The team expands Product Backlog Items into more explicit tasks on a
Sprint Backlog and then manages its own work and self -organises
around how it desires to complete the iteration.
✓ Typically 6 - 12 people
✓ Cross-functional to all the necessary
tasks to be handled (for example,
including programmers, architect,
designers, QA/testers, tech. writer,
…)
✓ Self-organised and self-directed
✓ Owns the project
It will only work if the team members are
✓ Experienced
✓ Motivated
✓ Committed to the project
✓ Proud of their work
✓ Able to work well with others
✓ Responsible
✓ Willing to work in teams
✓ Autonomous

before the Sprint Planning Meeting

RE in Agile projects: User stories
Agile:
• Lightweight approach to requirements engineering, project
management and other aspects of software development
• Business Analyst is often the Developer ☺
User stories:
• Less detailed that “classical requirements specification”
• But still be written and provide some product
documentation
• Seek to combine the strengths of written and verbal
communication, where possible supported by a picture.
• Written in user language
As a [user role]
I want to [goal action]
So that  [reason/benefit]
Example:
As a registered user, I want to
change my password so that
I can keep it secure or make it
easier to remember.
===== Page 26 =====
User Stories
• Deliberately leave out a lot of important details: Help getting the
high-level picture of requirements (provide a “bone” for eliciting
during scrum meetings)
• Product Backlog is evolving during the whole length of the project.
• Elaborated and modified through the project based on user input
User stories are very flexible and can range from individual
functional requirements, scenarios, use cases, product
features, business objectives, constraints, quality attributes,
business rules, user interface issues, and desired
characteristics of the product
• Centered on the result and the benefit of the thing you're
describing
• User- and usage-centric requirements elicitation:
focus on what the user wants to do, and not what the user wants
the system to do:
By focusing on the user’s goal and vision, you can ensure that the
users get the functionality that they require

User stories: Core characteristics
• Are not a contract, not detailed specifications.
• Are a kind of “notes” to remind the team on to discuss and collaborate
to clarify the details near the time of development
• High level, no details, as the goal is
• To avoid a false impressions of precision or completeness
• To highlight that further discussions are a part of the process
• Should be as independent as possible
• Dependencies might lead to difficulties with prioritizing and planning
• Should be valuable to the user (or owner) of the product – like
traditional req. specification  should contain only necessary requirements

===== Page 28 =====
User stories: Core characteristics
• Need to provide enough information to estimate, without being
too detailed.
• Should be “small enough”: small in the sense of the feature
scope, not in the sense “very short text”
• Should be detailed enough for the team to start work from, and
further details can be established and clarified at the time of
development.
• Generally: A team must be able to finish a user story within the
sprint in which it starts
• Should provide enough details of how the User Story will be
tested.

# investment-analysis.md — Investment Analysis Playbook

This playbook defines how investment opportunities should be analyzed.

The goal is to produce rational, structured, and decision-useful analysis.

Avoid emotional reasoning, hype, and shallow narratives.

---

# Core Objective

When evaluating an investment, determine:

1. what the asset or business is
2. how it creates value
3. what could make it grow
4. what could make it fail
5. whether the risk/reward is attractive

The goal is not just to find upside.

The goal is to make sound decisions under uncertainty.

---

# Analysis Principles

Always prioritize:

- clarity
- evidence
- incentives
- downside awareness
- expected value
- long-term thinking

Avoid:

- hype-driven conclusions
- blind optimism
- overconfidence
- vague narratives without numbers
- ignoring downside risk

---

# Standard Analysis Process

For every investment idea, follow this order:

1. Define the asset
2. Understand the business or system
3. Identify the value driver
4. Analyze market structure
5. Evaluate competitive position
6. Assess financial or economic strength
7. Identify catalysts
8. Evaluate risks
9. Estimate upside vs downside
10. Form a conclusion

Do not skip directly to “buy” or “don’t buy”.

---

# Step 1 — Define the Asset

Clarify exactly what is being analyzed.

Questions:

- Is this a stock, crypto asset, private business, startup, ETF, commodity, or other asset?
- What does it represent?
- What does the buyer actually own?
- What determines its value?

Output:

- asset type
- ownership structure
- source of value

---

# Step 2 — Understand the Business or System

Explain how the asset works in plain language.

For companies:

- What does the company sell?
- Who pays them?
- Why do customers buy?
- How does money flow through the business?

For crypto:

- What does the protocol do?
- Who uses it?
- Why does the token exist?
- Is the token actually necessary?

Output:

- simple explanation of the system
- value creation mechanism
- dependency map

---

# Step 3 — Identify the Core Value Driver

Find the main reason the asset could become more valuable.

Possible drivers:

- revenue growth
- earnings growth
- margin expansion
- market share gains
- network effects
- scarcity
- increased adoption
- improved incentives
- operating leverage
- asset rerating

Questions:

- What is the primary growth engine?
- What metric matters most?
- What would need to go right?

Output:

- top 1-3 value drivers
- key metrics to watch

---

# Step 4 — Analyze Market Structure

Understand the environment the asset operates in.

Questions:

- How large is the market?
- Is the market growing or shrinking?
- Is it fragmented or concentrated?
- What are the barriers to entry?
- Is the industry cyclical, defensive, or structurally growing?

For crypto:

- Is usage real or speculative?
- Are users sticky?
- Is there strong competition from other protocols?

Output:

- market size
- growth trend
- structure
- industry type

---

# Step 5 — Evaluate Competitive Position

Determine whether the asset has an advantage.

Questions:

- Why would customers choose this over alternatives?
- Does it have brand, technology, distribution, cost, speed, regulation, liquidity, or network advantage?
- Is the advantage durable?
- Can competitors copy it easily?

Possible moats:

- network effects
- switching costs
- low-cost advantage
- brand strength
- proprietary technology
- regulation / licenses
- ecosystem lock-in

Output:

- strengths
- weaknesses
- moat assessment
- durability of advantage

---

# Step 6 — Assess Financial or Economic Strength

For public companies, analyze financial quality.

Key areas:

- revenue growth
- margins
- profitability
- free cash flow
- debt load
- return on capital
- cash position
- dilution risk

Questions:

- Is the business financially healthy?
- Is growth profitable or fragile?
- Can it survive downturns?
- Is management allocating capital intelligently?

For crypto or early-stage systems, evaluate economic design:

- token issuance
- inflation / dilution
- incentive alignment
- fee generation
- treasury strength
- validator / participant incentives
- sustainability of rewards

Output:

- financial quality
- survivability
- capital efficiency
- dilution or balance sheet risk

---

# Step 7 — Identify Catalysts

Catalysts are events that may change market perception or fundamentals.

Examples:

- earnings growth
- product launch
- regulatory approval
- cost reduction
- margin improvement
- new partnerships
- adoption milestones
- token unlock completion
- macro tailwinds
- valuation rerating

Questions:

- What could cause the market to notice?
- What milestones would materially improve the thesis?
- What timing factors matter?

Output:

- near-term catalysts
- medium-term catalysts
- catalyst probability

---

# Step 8 — Evaluate Risks

This is one of the most important steps.

Always identify:

- business risk
- market risk
- competitive risk
- execution risk
- balance sheet risk
- regulatory risk
- governance risk
- liquidity risk
- valuation risk
- downside scenario risk

For crypto, also check:

- smart contract risk
- governance capture
- token dilution
- weak token utility
- security failures
- bridge risk
- ecosystem dependency

Questions:

- What could break the thesis?
- What is the most likely failure mode?
- What could cause permanent capital loss?
- What assumptions are fragile?

Output:

- major risks
- severity
- likelihood
- early warning signs

---

# Step 9 — Estimate Upside vs Downside

Frame the opportunity probabilistically.

Use scenario thinking when possible:

- bear case
- base case
- bull case

Questions:

- If things go badly, how much downside exists?
- If things go reasonably well, what is fair value?
- If things go very well, what is the upside?
- Is the reward worth the risk?

When possible, estimate:

- expected value
- risk/reward ratio
- margin of safety

Output:

- downside case
- base case
- upside case
- asymmetry assessment

---

# Step 10 — Form a Conclusion

Conclude in a way that supports decision-making.

Final output should answer:

- What is the thesis?
- Why might this work?
- What are the biggest risks?
- What would invalidate the thesis?
- Is this attractive now, later, or not at all?

Possible conclusion formats:

- attractive
- watchlist
- too early
- too risky
- overvalued
- misunderstood opportunity

---

# Required Output Format

When presenting an investment analysis, structure it like this:

## Investment Summary
- asset
- sector
- short thesis

## What It Is
- plain-language explanation

## Value Drivers
- top drivers of upside

## Market Structure
- market size, competition, industry dynamics

## Competitive Position
- strengths, weaknesses, moat

## Financial / Economic Quality
- health, profitability, incentives, sustainability

## Catalysts
- near- and medium-term triggers

## Risks
- main failure modes and warning signs

## Scenario View
- bear / base / bull

## Final View
- conclusion and reasoning

---

# Special Rules

## Rule 1 — No Hype
Never confuse popularity with value.

## Rule 2 — No Blind Conviction
Strong opinions must be supported by evidence.

## Rule 3 — Downside First
Always understand what can go wrong before focusing on upside.

## Rule 4 — Incentives Matter
Always ask who benefits, who pays, and whether incentives align.

## Rule 5 — Simplicity Wins
If the thesis cannot be explained clearly, it is probably not understood well enough.

# Asset-Specific Notes

## For Stocks
Pay special attention to:

- revenue growth quality
- margin trend
- free cash flow
- debt
- valuation multiples
- management quality
- industry cyclicality

## For Crypto
Pay special attention to:

- token utility
- tokenomics
- real demand vs speculative demand
- ecosystem dependency
- governance risks
- emissions / unlocks
- security and protocol design

## For Startups / Private Businesses
Pay special attention to:

- founder quality
- product-market fit
- burn rate
- runway
- market size
- defensibility
- path to sustainable economics

---

# Red Flags

Immediate caution if you see:

- unclear business model
- no real customer demand
- growth without economics
- heavy dilution
- unsustainable incentives
- excessive leverage
- management credibility issues
- valuation based only on narrative
- no identifiable moat
- dependence on one fragile assumption

---

# Decision Standard

A good investment is not just “a good story.”

A good investment usually has:

- understandable value creation
- credible upside driver
- manageable downside
- tolerable risk
- acceptable valuation
- clear thesis invalidation points

If these are missing, move the asset to watchlist or reject it.

---

# Review Process

Before finalizing analysis, check:

1. Did I explain the asset clearly?
2. Did I identify the real value driver?
3. Did I examine downside seriously?
4. Did I separate facts from assumptions?
5. Did I avoid hype and narrative traps?
6. Did I provide a usable conclusion?

If not, refine the analysis.

---

# Final Principle

Investing is decision-making under uncertainty.

The goal is not certainty.

The goal is disciplined judgment, strong risk control, and intelligent positioning.

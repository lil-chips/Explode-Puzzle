# mental-models.md — Core Mental Models

This document defines fundamental thinking models used to analyze complex systems.

Mental models are frameworks that help simplify reality and improve decision-making.

No single model explains everything.  
Use multiple models to gain a clearer perspective.

---

# First Principles Thinking

Break problems down to their fundamental truths.

Instead of reasoning by analogy, ask:

- What must be true?
- What assumptions exist?
- What are the basic physical or economic constraints?

Rebuild understanding from the ground up.

Use this model when:

- facing new or complex problems
- evaluating innovative ideas
- challenging conventional assumptions

---

# Systems Thinking

Many problems exist within systems of interconnected components.

Questions:

- What are the key components of the system?
- How do they interact?
- What feedback loops exist?

Systems often behave differently than individual parts.

Use this model when analyzing:

- markets
- economies
- technological ecosystems
- organizations

---

# Incentives

People and systems respond strongly to incentives.

Ask:

- Who benefits?
- Who pays the cost?
- What behaviors are being rewarded?
- What behaviors are being discouraged?

Misaligned incentives often explain irrational outcomes.

Use this model when evaluating:

- businesses
- markets
- governance structures
- organizational behavior

---

# Feedback Loops

Feedback loops influence system behavior.

### Positive feedback

Amplifies changes.

Examples:

- network effects
- viral adoption
- speculative bubbles

### Negative feedback

Stabilizes systems.

Examples:

- regulatory controls
- market corrections
- resource constraints

Understanding feedback loops helps predict system behavior.

---

# Network Effects

A network effect occurs when a system becomes more valuable as more people use it.

Examples:

- social networks
- payment networks
- blockchain ecosystems

Questions:

- Does value increase with user growth?
- Is the network difficult to replicate?

Strong network effects can create powerful competitive advantages.

---

# Margin of Safety

This concept comes from value investing.

The idea is to leave room for error.

Questions:

- What if assumptions are wrong?
- What if conditions change unexpectedly?

A margin of safety reduces risk.

Use this model in:

- investing
- strategic planning
- resource allocation

---

# Opportunity Cost

Every decision involves trade-offs.

Choosing one option means giving up another.

Questions:

- What alternatives are being sacrificed?
- Is this the best use of time, capital, or attention?

Opportunity cost thinking helps prioritize resources.

---

# Probabilistic Thinking

Many outcomes are uncertain.

Instead of asking:

"Will this happen?"

Ask:

"What is the probability of this happening?"

Think in terms of:

- probabilities
- expected value
- ranges of outcomes

This model is essential in:

- investing
- strategic decisions
- risk management

---

# Compounding

Small improvements can accumulate into large results over time.

Examples:

- compound interest
- learning accumulation
- network growth

Questions:

- Does this system compound over time?
- Are gains reinvested?

Compounding is one of the most powerful forces in long-term growth.

---

# Second-Order Thinking

Most people focus on immediate effects.

Better thinkers consider:

- second-order consequences
- unintended side effects

Questions:

- What happens next?
- What are the downstream effects?

Second-order thinking helps avoid short-sighted decisions.

---

# Asymmetric Risk / Reward

Some opportunities offer limited downside but large upside.

This is called positive asymmetry.

Questions:

- What is the worst-case outcome?
- What is the best-case outcome?
- Is the upside much larger than the downside?

Investors often seek opportunities with asymmetric payoff structures.

---

# Complexity vs Simplicity

Complex systems are harder to predict and control.

Whenever possible:

- simplify systems
- reduce unnecessary dependencies
- prefer robust designs

Simple solutions are often more reliable.

---

# Survivorship Bias

Sometimes we only observe successful outcomes.

This can distort conclusions.

Questions:

- What failures are invisible?
- Are we ignoring unsuccessful examples?

Understanding survivorship bias prevents misleading analysis.

---

# Path Dependence

The future of a system often depends on its past decisions.

Early choices can constrain future possibilities.

Questions:

- What historical decisions shaped the current system?
- What lock-in effects exist?

Path dependence explains why systems evolve in certain directions.

---

# Final Principle

Mental models are tools for thinking.

No single model explains reality completely.

Better thinking comes from combining multiple models to understand complex systems.

# decision-making.md — Strategic Decision-Making Playbook

This playbook defines how important decisions should be evaluated.

The goal is to reduce emotional bias, improve clarity, and make decisions with structured reasoning.

Decisions should be based on incentives, probabilities, and consequences.

---

# Core Principle

A good decision is not defined only by the outcome.

A good decision is one that was made with:

- clear reasoning
- proper risk awareness
- realistic assumptions
- disciplined evaluation of alternatives

Focus on decision quality, not short-term results.

---

# Step 1 — Define the Decision

Clearly state the decision being made.

Questions:

- What decision needs to be made?
- What is the time horizon?
- Is the decision reversible or irreversible?

Types of decisions:

- reversible decisions (can be changed later)
- irreversible decisions (long-term consequences)

Reversible decisions should be made quickly.

Irreversible decisions require deeper analysis.

Output:

- clear decision statement
- decision type
- urgency level

---

# Step 2 — Define the Objective

Clarify what success looks like.

Questions:

- What is the primary objective?
- What metrics determine success?
- What constraints exist?

Avoid vague goals.

Examples:

- maximize long-term returns
- reduce downside risk
- improve system reliability
- accelerate learning

Output:

- primary objective
- success criteria

---

# Step 3 — Gather Relevant Information

Identify the key facts needed for the decision.

Questions:

- What information is essential?
- What information is missing?
- What assumptions are being made?

Avoid analysis paralysis.

Focus on information that materially affects the outcome.

Output:

- known facts
- key assumptions
- unknown variables

---

# Step 4 — Identify Options

List realistic alternatives.

Avoid false binary choices.

Example structure:

Option A — conservative approach  
Option B — moderate risk  
Option C — aggressive approach  

Questions:

- What are the realistic alternatives?
- What trade-offs exist?

Output:

- list of options
- brief description of each

---

# Step 5 — Evaluate Consequences

Consider potential outcomes for each option.

Questions:

- What are the best-case outcomes?
- What are the worst-case outcomes?
- What are the most likely outcomes?

Think in terms of probability.

Output:

- possible scenarios
- expected consequences

---

# Step 6 — Risk Analysis

Evaluate risks before focusing on rewards.

Questions:

- What could go wrong?
- What could cause irreversible damage?
- What risks are acceptable?

Types of risks:

- financial risk
- operational risk
- reputational risk
- opportunity cost
- systemic risk

Output:

- major risks
- probability
- severity

---

# Step 7 — Opportunity Cost

Every decision means rejecting alternatives.

Questions:

- What opportunities are lost if this option is chosen?
- Is the chosen path the best use of time and capital?

Output:

- opportunity cost assessment

---

# Step 8 — Expected Value Thinking

Estimate the value of each option.

Expected value =

probability × outcome impact

Questions:

- which option has the best long-term payoff?
- which option has asymmetric reward vs risk?

Focus on decisions with positive asymmetry.

Output:

- relative attractiveness of options

---

# Step 9 — Reversibility Check

Before finalizing a decision, ask:

- Can this decision be reversed?
- How costly would reversal be?
- Is it safer to experiment first?

Prefer reversible experimentation when uncertainty is high.

Output:

- reversibility assessment

---

# Step 10 — Decision

Choose the best option based on reasoning.

The final decision should clearly state:

- chosen option
- reasoning
- risks accepted
- assumptions made

Output format:

Decision:  
Reasoning:  
Key risks:  
Conditions for review:

---

# Step 11 — Post-Decision Review

After implementation, evaluate the result.

Questions:

- Was the reasoning sound?
- Were assumptions correct?
- What lessons were learned?

The goal is continuous improvement in decision quality.

Output:

- lessons learned
- adjustments for future decisions

---

# Common Cognitive Biases

Be aware of common mental traps.

### Confirmation Bias

Only seeking information that supports existing beliefs.

### Recency Bias

Overweighting recent events.

### Overconfidence

Assuming too much certainty.

### Loss Aversion

Avoiding losses even when risk is justified.

### Herd Behavior

Following crowd sentiment.

Recognizing biases improves decision quality.

---

# Decision Categories

Different decisions require different levels of analysis.

### Low-impact decisions

Make quickly.

### Medium-impact decisions

Use structured analysis.

### High-impact decisions

Use full framework and careful risk assessment.

---

# Final Principle

Perfect decisions do not exist.

The goal is to consistently make **better decisions than average** by using disciplined thinking and structured analysis.
